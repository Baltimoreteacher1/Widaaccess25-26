What do all these numbers actually mean?

    
Start here if anything in this dashboard feels confusing.

    
🌡️
Think of it like a doctor's visit
ACCESS scores are like a thermometer reading — they measure English language proficiency at one point in time. Growth is how much the temperature changed. The target is the healthy range we're aiming for. The gap tells you how far from that target the student is.

    
Every student has two scores

    
Each student took ACCESS last year (prior score) and this year (current score). From those two numbers, we calculate everything else:

    

      Growth  = Current score − Prior score

      Gap     = Current score − Target score

      Met target? = Yes if Gap ≥ 0, No if Gap < 0

      Near miss   = Gap is between −0.2 and 0
    

    
🏃
Example
A student had a prior score of 3.1, a current score of 3.4, and a target of 3.5.

    Growth = 3.4 − 3.1 = +0.3 (they grew!)

    Gap = 3.4 − 3.5 = −0.1 (missed by 0.1 — near miss)

    Met target? No — but they're very close.

    
What ACCESS scores represent

    

    
Scores go from 1.0 (just starting) to 6.0 (fully proficient). Each student's growth target is personalized — someone at 1.5 is expected to grow more in a year than someone already at 4.2.

    
Important: ACCESS measures English language proficiency — not intelligence, effort, or academic ability. A student can be brilliant and still have a low ACCESS score if they're new to English.

  







  

    
Met target % — what it means

    
The percentage of students who reached their personalized growth goal this year.

    
🎯
Like a track team's personal bests
Each runner has their own target time — not the same for everyone. "Met target %" counts how many runners beat their own personal target. A runner who improved from 90 seconds to 85 seconds "met target" even if another runner runs 60 seconds. It's about personal growth, not comparison.

Why elementary is higher than middle

    
This is mostly not about instruction quality. It reflects: (1) earlier-year learners have lower targets that are easier to reach; (2) middle school has a large concentration of LTML students with very high targets; (3) different grade compositions. The elementary–middle gap (61% vs 26%) is statistically very significant (p < .001, odds ratio = 4.49), but comparing raw percentages without that context is misleading.

    
The odds ratio of 4.49 means elementary students are 4.5× more likely to meet their target than middle school students — driven largely by structural factors, not teaching quality differences. 

  







  

    
Growth and Gap — the two most useful numbers

    
They look similar but they answer different questions.

    

      
📈
Growth = how much did they change?
Current score minus prior score. A student who went from 2.5 to 3.2 has growth of +0.7. A student who went from 3.8 to 3.4 has growth of −0.4 (declined).

      
🎯
Gap = how far from the goal?
Current score minus their target. A gap of −0.1 means they just missed. A gap of +0.5 means they exceeded the goal by half a point.

Which one matters more for planning?

    

      
Use GAP to prioritize
Gap tells you who to put in which group. Near miss (gap −0.1 to −0.2) = quick win. Large gap (−0.8 or worse) = sustained support.

      
Use GROWTH for diagnosis
Growth tells you the direction. If a student declined, investigate what changed before assigning more practice. Growth shows momentum.

    

    
Declined students need barrier review first. Ask: Did attendance change? Did the student disengage from the test? Is there a domain weakness (speaking vs. writing)? Add practice only after identifying the barrier — otherwise you're adding work on top of an unsolved problem.

  







  

    
Confidence Intervals — how much should you trust a percentage?

    
Every percentage has an uncertainty range. This tells you how wide it is.

    
🎣
Like a weather forecast
When the weather app says "70% chance of rain," it doesn't mean exactly 70% — it means "somewhere in the range of 60–80%." A confidence interval works the same way. The percentage is our best estimate; the CI tells you the likely range around it.

    
What a 95% CI actually means

    
If we could magically re-run the same school year many times, 95% of those repeated observations would produce a result inside this range. It reflects how stable the number is given the group size.

    
How group size affects confidence

    
The bigger the group, the narrower the interval, the more you can trust the number.

    

    
The formula (optional)

    

95% CI = p ± 1.96 × √(p × (1−p) / n)

School example: p=49.3%, n=347
  CI = 49.3% ± (1.96 × 2.7%) = [44.1%, 54.5%]
  → The true rate is very likely between 44% and 55%.

Grade 7 LTML: p=0%, n=16
  CI = [0%, 20.6%] — we can't be confident even in the 0%.

    
Rule of thumb: Never make instructional decisions based on a percentage from a group smaller than 20 students without noting that the CI is wide. The number might look exact but isn't.

  







  

    
Correlation (r) — seeing patterns between two things

    
One of the most useful — and most misunderstood — numbers in data analysis.

    
☔
Like noticing that dark clouds often come before rain
Correlation means two things tend to happen together. But correlation doesn't tell you why — dark clouds don't cause rain, they just co-occur with it. Same with r: it shows a pattern, not a cause.

    
The r scale — from "opposite" to "together"

    

    

    
What the numbers mean in plain English

    

      
+0.75
Strong positive — as one goes up, the other clearly goes up too. Like "students who scored higher last year tend to score higher this year."

      
+0.20
Small positive — a real but weak upward pattern. Useful for asking questions, not for making decisions.

      
0.00
No relationship — knowing one tells you nothing about the other.

      
−0.36
Small negative — as one goes up, the other tends to go down. In our data: the more growth a student needed, the less likely they were to meet target.

    

    
r² — the really useful number

    
Square the correlation to see how much of the outcome it actually explains. This is usually much less impressive than r looks:

    

r = +0.752  →  r² = 56.6%  →  prior score explains 56.6% of this year's score
r = −0.356  →  r² = 12.7%  →  needed growth explains 12.7% of whether target was met
r = +0.109  →  r² =  1.2%  →  prior score explains only 1.2% of meeting target

    
The key caution for this data

    
Several numbers here are mathematically linked — growth, gap, met target, and current score are all calculated from the same two scores. Some correlations are strong partly because of how the numbers are defined, not because of a real-world relationship. Always check the caution note on each insight card.

    

      
✅ Use r to…
  • Notice patterns worth investigating
  • Ask better questions at team meetings
  • Understand which variables move together

      
❌ Don't use r to…
  • Prove that one thing caused another
  • Make individual student decisions
  • Claim a finding "proves" anything

    

  







  

    
p-values — is this a real pattern or just luck?

    
p-values answer one specific question. They're often misread as answering a different one.

    
🎰
Like flipping a coin
If you flip a coin 10 times and get 8 heads, you might wonder if it's a fair coin. A p-value answers: "How often would a fair coin produce 8 or more heads just by luck?" If that answer is very small (p < .05), we say the result is unlikely to be pure chance.

    
The one thing p tells you

    
A small p-value (like p < .001) means: "If there were truly no relationship, random chance would produce a pattern this strong less than 0.1% of the time." In other words — this is probably a real pattern, not noise.

    
What p does NOT mean

    

      
❌ p does not mean "the effect is large." With 347 students, even a tiny correlation of r = .11 reaches p = .04. That's statistically significant but explains only 1.2% of the outcome — practically meaningless.

      
❌ p does not mean "97% chance the relationship is real." That's not what it measures. p is not a probability that the hypothesis is true.

      
✅ p < .05 means: This pattern is unlikely to be random noise given this sample size. That's all — nothing more.

    

    
Key statistical results for this school

    

      

        
Elementary vs. Middle met-target ratep < .001

        
χ² = 36.84. The gap between 61% (elementary) and 26% (middle) is statistically very real. Odds ratio = 4.49 — elementary students are 4.5× more likely to meet target. This is mostly structural, not instructional.

      

      

        
Grade × Met target ratep < .001

        
χ² = 76.11. Met-target rates differ significantly across grades. Grade 4 = 84%, Grade 7 = 7.9%. Grade 7 is heavily influenced by LTML students with high targets.

      

    

    
📏
The odds ratio explained simply
An odds ratio of 4.49 between elementary and middle means: for every middle school student who meets target, 4.49 elementary students meet theirs. It's like saying "elementary students have 4.5× better odds at a card game" — but the game rules (target structure) are different, not just the players.

  







  

    
Opportunity Score — how to prioritize

    
A planning index that combines multiple risk signals so you don't have to cross-reference five filters manually.

    
🚦
Like a traffic light that checks multiple things at once
Instead of checking speed, fuel, and engine separately, the opportunity score combines them into one signal. High score = multiple warning lights on at the same time. It doesn't mean the car is broken — it means check this one first.

    
What goes into the score

    

    
How to read it

    

      
75–100
Highest priority — multiple risk signals. Start here, but investigate the barrier before adding more work.

      
50–74
Elevated — plan for this student in the next cycle. Not urgent, but needs attention.

      
0–10
Met target — sustain what's working. Monitor, don't over-plan.

    

    
This is a planning tool, not a label. A student with a score of 90 is not "worse than" a student with 60 in any permanent sense. Teacher knowledge, relationships, and classroom context should always override the score. Reassign groups after every evidence cycle.

What this data can't tell you

    
Using data well means being honest about its limits.

    
🔦
Data is a flashlight, not a floodlight
A flashlight shows you what's in its beam — clearly, usefully. But it doesn't light up the whole room. ACCESS data gives you a clear picture of English language proficiency scores and growth. It doesn't show you everything that matters about a student.

    

      
1. ACCESS ≠ academic ability
A student can be excellent at math or science and have a low ACCESS score if they're still developing English. Don't use ACCESS scores to make academic placement decisions or form assumptions about cognitive ability.

      
2. Growth and targets aren't equal across students
A student who grew +0.8 and missed their target may be making better progress than a student who grew +0.1 and met their target — because their targets were very different. Compare students to their own targets, not to each other.

      
3. Year-to-year comparisons are tricky
Different students enroll each year. A drop from 53% to 49% might reflect a higher-need incoming class, not worse instruction. Look at trends alongside enrollment and demographic context.

      
4. Correlation is not causation — ever
The strongest correlation in this dataset (prior score → current score, r=.75) is mostly a stability effect — scores don't change dramatically year-to-year. It does not mean prior scores "cause" current scores, or that you can't influence this trajectory.

      
5. Teacher knowledge overrides any score
If a student has a high opportunity score but their teacher knows they were sick during testing or going through a family crisis, that context matters more than the number. Data is a starting point for conversation, not the end of it.

    

    
💬
The right framing for data meetings
"This pattern raises a question worth exploring together." Never: "The data proves we need to do X." Data shows patterns. Teachers understand causes. Both are needed.
