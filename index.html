What this dashboard measures

          
A plain-English map of every number you'll encounter.

          
Every number in this dashboard comes from two ACCESS test administrations for each student: their prior-year score (SY 2024–2025) and their current score (SY 2025–2026). From those two numbers, everything else is derived.

          
The five core derived values

          

Growth = Current score − Prior score
Gap = Current score − Target score  (positive = exceeded target)
Needed growth = Target − Prior score  (how much they needed to grow)
Met target = Gap ≥ 0  (true/false)
Opportunity score = composite planning index (see Opportunity Score tab)

          
Example: A student had a prior score of 3.1, a current score of 3.4, and a target of 3.5.

Growth = 3.4 − 3.1 = +0.3

Gap = 3.4 − 3.5 = −0.1 (missed by 0.1 — near miss)

Met target = false

          
What ACCESS scores mean

          
ACCESS proficiency scores range roughly from 1.0 to 6.0. They represent a student's English language proficiency on WIDA's composite scale. A score of 2.5 = Developing, 3.5 = Expanding, 4.5+ = Bridging/Reaching. Growth targets are personalized — a student at 1.5 is expected to grow more in a year than a student already at 4.2.

          
Important: ACCESS measures language proficiency, not academic achievement. A student can have strong content knowledge with a low ACCESS score, or vice versa. Do not use ACCESS scores alone to make academic placement decisions.

Correlation (r) explained

          
The most misused number in education data. Here's what it actually means.

          
Pearson's r measures how closely two variables follow a straight line when you plot one against the other. It ranges from −1 (perfectly opposite) to +1 (perfectly together), with 0 meaning no linear relationship at all.

          
Visual strength guide

          

            
−.75
Strong negative
As X goes up, Y clearly goes down

            
−.30
Small negative
Weak downward tendency

            
.00
No linear trend
Knowing X tells you nothing about Y

            
+.35
Small positive
Weak upward tendency

            
+.75
Strong positive
As X goes up, Y clearly goes up too

          

          
Standard strength thresholds

          
    
            
  • |r| < .20 — Very weak: barely detectable pattern. Do not use for decisions.
    • 
            
  • |r| .20–.39 — Small: a real but modest pattern. Good for generating questions.
    • 
            
  • |r| .40–.69 — Moderate: a meaningful pattern worth discussing with the team.
    • 
            
  • |r| ≥ .70 — Strong: a consistent linear relationship. Still not proof of causation.
    • 
          

          
The formula (for reference)

          
r = Σ[(xᵢ − x̄)(yᵢ − ȳ)] / √[Σ(xᵢ − x̄)² × Σ(yᵢ − ȳ)²]
Where: x̄ = mean of x, ȳ = mean of y, Σ = sum across all n students

          
What r² (R-squared) means

          
Square the correlation to get the proportion of variance explained. This is more useful for sizing the practical meaning:

          
r = .356 → r² = .127 → prior score explains ~13% of variance in meeting target
r = .752 → r² = .565 → prior score explains ~57% of variance in current score

          
The remaining variance (87% and 43% respectively) comes from factors not in this dataset — teacher practice, attendance, family context, test engagement, etc.

          
Three things r cannot tell you

          

            
❌ Causation
r = .752 between prior and current score does NOT mean the prior score caused the current score. Both reflect the same underlying skill.

            
❌ Direction of influence
A correlation between grade level and growth doesn't tell you whether grade causes growth pattern or growth pattern defines grade placement.

            
❌ Non-linear relationships
Pearson r only detects straight-line patterns. Two variables can have a strong curved relationship and still show r ≈ 0.

          

          
The mathematical overlap caution (critical for ACCESS data)

          
Several ACCESS variables are mathematically connected. Growth = Current − Prior. Gap = Current − Target. Met target = Gap ≥ 0. This means correlations between these variables are partly definitional, not purely empirical. For example, "needed growth" and "met target" are correlated largely because both are derived from the same target score — not because needed growth directly predicts meeting it. Always read the caution on each insight card.

        

      


      
      

        

          
Confidence Intervals explained

          
The range that tells you how much to trust a percentage.

          
A 95% confidence interval (CI) is a range around a percentage that says: "If we could observe this group many times, 95% of those observations would produce a result within this range."

          
In plain terms: it tells you how precise your number is. A wide CI means the number is uncertain. A narrow CI means you can trust it.

          
The formula used in this dashboard

          
For a proportion p with sample size n:
  Standard Error (SE) = √[p × (1−p) / n]
  95% CI = p ± 1.96 × SE

Example: 49.3% met target, n = 347
  SE = √[0.493 × 0.507 / 347] = √[0.000721] = 0.0268
  95% CI = 0.493 ± (1.96 × 0.0268) = 0.493 ± 0.053
  → [44.1%, 54.5%]

          
What different group sizes mean

          

            
nMet rate95% CIInterpretation

            
              
347 (full school)49.3%[44.1% – 54.5%]±5 points. Reliable for reporting.

              
44 (Grade 1)31.8%[18.4% – 45.2%]±13 points. Wide. Handle with caution.

              
16 (Grade 7 LTML)0%[0% – 20.6%]Very wide. The 0% is consistent but small n.

              
4 (small cell)75%[32.6% – 100%]Almost meaningless — CI is 67 points wide.

            
          

          
Rule of thumb: Don't make instructional decisions based on percentages from groups smaller than 20 students without noting the confidence interval. The number might look precise but isn't.

          
Why the heatmap dims cells with n < 10

          
Any cell with fewer than 10 students has a CI so wide that the displayed percentage could easily be off by 30 or more percentage points. The reduced opacity signals: "This is a real number, but interpret with significant caution."

        

      


      
      

        

          
p-values explained

          
The most misunderstood number in statistics. Here's what it actually says — and doesn't say.

          
A p-value answers one very specific question: "If there were truly no relationship between these two variables in the real world, how likely is it that random chance alone would produce a result as extreme as the one I observed?"

          
A p-value of .03 means: if the null hypothesis (no relationship) were true, you'd see a result this extreme only 3% of the time by chance. By convention, p < .05 is called "statistically significant."

          
The critical misunderstanding

          

            
❌ What p does NOT mean
"There is a 97% chance the relationship is real."
"The effect is large or important."
"This finding proves anything about causation."
"The finding will replicate in another school."

            
✅ What p DOES mean
"This pattern is unlikely to be pure random noise given this sample size."
A small p with a small r still means a small, possibly irrelevant, effect.
p only screens for noise — not for importance.

          

          
Why large datasets inflate significance

          
With n = 347 students, even a correlation of r = .109 reaches p = .043. But r = .109 means the predictor explains only 1.2% of the variance in the outcome. That's statistically significant but practically negligible.

          
Effect size (r²): r = .109 → r² = .012 → predictor explains 1.2% of variance
This is real (not noise) but is a tiny effect — not a planning signal.

          
The chi-square statistics in this dataset

          
Two chi-square tests were run on this data:

          
    
            
  • Band × Met target (χ² = 36.84, p < .001): Elementary vs. middle school met-target rates differ significantly. Elementary = 61%, Middle = 25.9%. Odds ratio = 4.49 — elementary students are 4.5× more likely to meet target than middle school students.
    • 
            
  • Target year × Met target (χ² = 66.97, p < .001): Met-target rates differ significantly across target-year groups. LTML = 2.9%, Year 5 = 76.6%.
    • 
            
  • Grade × Met target (χ² = 76.11, p < .001): Met-target rates differ significantly across grades. Grade 4 = 84%, Grade 7 = 7.9%.
    • 
          

          
What the odds ratio means: An odds ratio of 4.49 means elementary students have 4.49× higher odds of meeting target compared to middle school students. This does NOT mean elementary teachers are 4.5× better — it reflects very different target structures, student populations, and expected growth rates across the school.

        

      


      
      

        

          
Growth & Gap explained

          
The two most important student-level numbers in this dashboard.

          
Growth

          
Growth = Current ACCESS score − Prior ACCESS score
Range in this dataset: −1.7 to +2.5
Mean: +0.54  |  Median: approximately +0.5

          
Growth is the raw change in proficiency points. It tells you how much a student changed but doesn't say whether that was enough. A student who grew +0.4 from 1.8 to 2.2 may have missed target, while a student who grew +0.4 from 4.1 to 4.5 may have met it.

          

            

              
            

          

          
Gap (the more important planning number)

          
Gap = Current score − Target score
Positive gap → Met target (exceeded)
Zero gap → Met target exactly
Negative gap → Did not meet target
Near miss → Gap between −0.2 and 0 (exclusive)

          
Gap vs. Growth: A student with growth = +0.8 and gap = −0.3 grew significantly but still didn't meet target (their target was high). A student with growth = +0.1 and gap = +0.2 barely grew but still met target (their target was low). Gap matters more for priority-setting. Growth matters more for barrier review.

          
When to investigate growth vs. gap

          
    
            
  • Negative growth (declined): Don't assign more practice. Investigate what changed. Check attendance, test engagement, transition, or domain.
    • 
            
  • Positive growth + negative gap (missed but grew): Student is moving in the right direction. A targeted sprint on the specific language function may close the gap.
    • 
            
  • Zero or minimal growth + negative gap: More investigation needed. What barrier is blocking language development?
    • 
            
  • Strong growth + positive gap (met + exceeded): Sustain and extend. Introduce precision tasks and leadership language roles.
    •

Opportunity Score explained

          
A planning index, not an official metric. Here's exactly how it's built.

          
The opportunity score is a composite index designed to help teachers prioritize which students to focus on in a short cycle. It combines multiple risk signals so you don't have to manually cross-reference five different filters.

          
How it's calculated (simplified)

          
Opportunity = weighted sum of risk signals:
  + 30 pts: student did not meet target
  + 15 pts: student declined (growth < 0)  
  + 20 pts: student is LTML
  + 15 pts: current score below 2.5 (foundational)
  + 10 pts: gap below −0.5 (large deficit)
  + 10 pts: near miss (gap between −0.2 and 0)
  
Range: 0 (met target, no risk signals) to 100 (all signals present)
Students who met target typically score 0–10.
Highest-priority students typically score 75–100.

          
This is a planning tool, not an official metric. The weights are heuristic — they encode one reasonable prioritization model. A teacher who knows a student's classroom behavior, attendance, or family context should always override the score. A student with opportunity = 60 is not "worse than" a student with 80 in any absolute sense — both need attention.

          
Distribution of opportunity scores in this dataset

          
Based on the 347 assessed students:

          
    
            
  • Score 0–10 (met target / low risk): ~171 students (~49%)
    • 
            
  • Score 11–50 (moderate signals): ~50 students (~14%)
    • 
            
  • Score 51–74 (elevated signals): ~46 students (~13%)
    • 
            
  • Score 75–100 (highest priority, multiple signals): ~80 students (~23%)
    • 
          

          
Recommended use: Sort the student table by opportunity score, take the top 8–12, and verify with teacher knowledge before forming the group. The score is a starting point — not a ranking for evaluation purposes.

When cells are missing (dashes)

          
A dash means no students in this school are in that grade/target-year combination. That's normal — Grade 1 students can't be in Year 6, and Grade 8 students rarely appear in Year 2.

          
Never compare cells with very different n values directly. A cell showing 100% with n=4 means 4 students met target — not that 100% attainment is typical for that group. Always check the n shown below each percentage.

        

      


      
      

        

          
What this data can't tell you

          
Using data well means knowing its limits as clearly as its strengths.

          
1. ACCESS measures language proficiency — not learning, effort, or teaching quality

          
A low ACCESS score reflects a student's current English language proficiency on a single test administration. It does not measure how hard the student worked, how effectively they were taught, their academic ability, or their growth in content knowledge. Do not use ACCESS scores as a proxy for cognitive capacity or potential.

          
2. Growth targets are personalized — cross-student comparison requires caution

          
Student A with growth +0.3 who met target and Student B with growth +0.9 who also met target are not "equally successful" — their targets were different. Similarly, a student with growth +0.5 who missed target was not less successful than a student with growth +0.2 who met target if the first student had a much higher target to hit.

          
3. Year-to-year cohort changes make trend comparisons tricky

          
The trend chart compares the same building across years, but the students are different each year (different students enter and leave the school). A drop from 53% to 49% does not necessarily mean instruction got worse — it may reflect a higher-need incoming cohort, a shift in target-year distribution, or other enrollment changes.

          
4. Correlation ≠ causation (repeated for emphasis)

          
The strongest correlation in this dataset is prior score → current score (r = .752). This is largely a stability effect — scores don't change dramatically year-to-year — not evidence that prior score "causes" current score. Classroom interventions that change academic language access can shift this trajectory, but r alone can't show that.

          
5. This is census data from one school, not a random sample

          
Because we're looking at all assessed students in one school, the confidence intervals are technically about measurement uncertainty, not sampling uncertainty. The 95% CI reflects how stable the percentage would be if we could rerun the same year — it does not generalize to other schools or districts.

          
6. The opportunity score is a planning heuristic

          
The weights in the opportunity score were chosen to be reasonable, not validated through research. A different weighting scheme would produce different rankings. Treat it as a sorting tool that surfaces patterns, not a precise measurement.

          
The right framing for data meetings: "This pattern in the data raises a question worth investigating." Not: "The data proves X." Data is always the start of a conversation, never the end of one. Teacher knowledge, relationship context, and classroom observation always have veto power over any dashboard metric.
